We Acknowledge the Indigenous Lands

Before it was ever “discovered” by European Americans, the Yellowstone region was home to many Indigenous peoples. The Native tribes lived, traveled, hunted, and held spiritual ceremonies throughout the area. These lands were rich with stories, traditions, and cultural significance.

We honor the following tribes whose history, culture, and presence are deeply connected to this landscape:

  • Shoshone-Bannock Tribes
  • Blackfeet Nation
  • Confederated Salish and Kootenai Tribes
  • Crow Tribe of Montana
  • Northern Arapaho Tribe
  • Northern Cheyenne Tribe
  • Eastern Shoshone Tribe
  • Kiowa Tribe
  • Nez Perce Tribe

Safety Tips for Exploring Yellowstone

Yellowstone’s beautiful landscapes can also present dangers. Follow these key tips to stay safe:

  • Stay on designated trails and boardwalks to avoid geothermal hazards.
  • Maintain a safe distance from wildlife. Bison, elk, and bears can be unpredictable.
  • Check weather forecasts before hiking or exploring.
  • Carry bear spray and know how to use it when exploring remote areas.
  • Always tell someone your plans before heading out on a trail.

For more detailed safety guidelines, visit the Yellowstone National Park Safety Guide.

Camping Rules & Fire Safety

Camping is a wonderful way to connect with Yellowstone’s natural beauty. Follow these important camping and fire safety rules:

Camping Rules & Grounds

  • Only camp in designated campgrounds.
  • Reserve your campsite early — Yellowstone campgrounds fill up quickly.
  • Store food properly to avoid attracting wildlife.
  • Keep your campsite clean and dispose of trash properly.
  • Check campground regulations for quiet hours and generator use.

Fire Safety

  • Fires are only allowed in designated fire rings or grills.
  • Never leave a fire unattended and fully extinguish it before leaving.
  • Follow all fire danger ratings and seasonal fire restrictions.

For detailed campground information, visit the Yellowstone Campground Guide.
